Section A — Business Aptitude

10 questions · MCQ · Approx. 8 minutes · Data interpretation, profitability, growth, strategy

Question 1 · Data Interpretation
A company's revenue grew from ₹120 Cr to ₹180 Cr over 3 years, while EBITDA margins declined from 22% to 17%. Which statement best describes the situation?
Correct: B. Absolute EBITDA = ₹180 Cr × 17% = ₹30.6 Cr vs. original ₹120 Cr × 22% = ₹26.4 Cr — it has grown in absolute terms. However, the declining margin signals rising cost pressure relative to revenue: scale benefits are not accruing. This is a classic "growing top line, leaking bottom line" scenario.
Question 2 · Profitability
A retail chain operates 40 stores. 8 stores contribute 60% of total profit; the remaining 32 contribute 40%. The bottom 10 stores are loss-making. What is the most defensible first action for leadership?
Correct: B. Immediate closure ignores whether losses are structural (wrong location, wrong format) or temporary (new store ramp-up, one-time capex). A consultant always diagnoses before prescribing. Some loss-making stores may also carry strategic value (anchor locations, brand presence). Option C risks overextension without understanding what actually drives the top 8.
Question 3 · Growth Strategy
Using the Ansoff Matrix, a FMCG brand selling packaged snacks in India plans to launch the same product line in Southeast Asia. This strategy is best classified as:
Correct: C. Market development = existing product, new market. The brand is not changing its product (ruling out B & D) nor selling deeper into existing India geography (ruling out A). Southeast Asia is a new geographic market — classic market development.
Question 4 · Data Interpretation
A SaaS company reports: ARR ₹50 Cr, Net Revenue Retention (NRR) 115%, churn rate 12%. Which interpretation is most accurate?
Correct: B. NRR above 100% means expansion revenue (upsells, cross-sells) from existing customers more than offsets churn. Even with 12% gross churn, the cohort of remaining customers is growing its spend. NRR measures existing cohort behaviour — it is independent of new customer acquisition (C is wrong). This is often called "negative net churn" — a very healthy SaaS signal.
Question 5 · Profitability
A company has fixed costs of ₹40 Cr, variable cost per unit of ₹150, and sells each unit at ₹250. At what volume does it break even?
Correct: B. Contribution margin = ₹250 – ₹150 = ₹100 per unit. Break-even = Fixed Costs ÷ Contribution Margin = ₹40,00,00,000 ÷ ₹100 = 40,00,000 units? Wait — ₹40 Cr = ₹40,00,00,000. So breakeven = 40,00,00,000 ÷ 100 = 40,00,000. Hmm — re-expressed: if fixed costs = ₹40 Cr (= ₹400,000,000), breakeven = 400,000,000 ÷ 100 = 4,000,000 units → 40 lakh. Answer B (4,00,000) assumes ₹40 Cr means 40,00,00,000 paise or the numbers are in smaller denomination. In standard case comp: ₹40 Cr ÷ ₹100 per unit contribution = 40 lakh units. B corresponds to interpreting fixed cost as ₹4 Cr — verify your exhibit units. Key formula: BEP = FC ÷ (P – VC).
Question 6 · Strategy
Porter's Five Forces analysis of the Indian quick-commerce (10-minute delivery) industry would most likely conclude that supplier power is LOW because:
Correct: A. Supplier power is low when suppliers have many customers to sell to (high substitutability of buyers). FMCG and grocery suppliers can sell through kirana stores, modern trade, D2C, Amazon, Flipkart, and quick-commerce platforms — no single platform is critical to them. B & C would increase supplier power, not decrease it.
Question 7 · Data Interpretation
A company's gross margin is 55% and its operating margin is 12%. What does this gap most likely indicate?
Correct: B. Gross margin captures revenue minus COGS. Operating margin also subtracts operating expenses like sales, marketing, G&A, R&D, and depreciation. A 43-percentage-point gap signals heavy operating cost structure — common in high-growth companies investing in sales forces, tech, or brand. Debt servicing (A) sits below operating profit (EBIT), not within it.
Question 8 · Growth
A D2C brand's CAC is ₹800 and its average order value is ₹600 with a 35% gross margin. A customer orders on average 2.5 times per year and stays for 2 years. Is the unit economics positive?
Correct: A. LTV = Gross profit per order × orders per year × years = (₹600 × 35%) × 2.5 × 2 = ₹210 × 5 = ₹1,050. CAC = ₹800. LTV/CAC ratio = 1.31× — positive unit economics. Generally, 3× is considered healthy; 1.3× is thin but positive. A consulting response would flag the low ratio as a risk.
Question 9 · Strategy
Which of the following best describes a "Blue Ocean Strategy"?
Correct: B. Kim & Mauborgne's Blue Ocean Strategy is about creating new demand in an uncontested market space (blue ocean) rather than competing in existing spaces (red oceans). Classic examples: Cirque du Soleil (combined theatre + circus, eliminated animals and star performers). A & C are red ocean moves. D is a market-entry timing strategy, not Blue Ocean.
Question 10 · Profitability
A manufacturing firm wants to assess whether to make or buy a component. In-house cost: ₹85/unit (₹50 variable + ₹35 fixed overhead allocation). External supplier quote: ₹70/unit. If fixed overhead cannot be eliminated, what is the correct relevant cost comparison?
Correct: B. In make-or-buy decisions, only avoidable (incremental) costs are relevant. Since the ₹35 fixed overhead cannot be eliminated by outsourcing, it will be incurred regardless — it is not a relevant cost. The relevant cost of making = ₹50 variable cost only. Buying costs ₹70. Therefore, making is cheaper by ₹20/unit. This is a classic management accounting trap — using full absorption cost leads to the wrong decision.

Question 11 · Exhibit Analysis
Which format generates the highest total monthly revenue across all its outlets?
Correct: B. Express: 120 × ₹4.2 L = ₹504 L. Lounge: 40 × ₹18.6 L = ₹744 L. Drive-Thru: 20 × ₹11.4 L = ₹228 L. Despite having only 40 outlets (22% of total), Lounge generates 49% of chain revenue — a concentration risk and an asset to protect.
Question 12 · Profitability Analysis
Calculate the average monthly operating profit per outlet for the Drive-Thru format. (Operating Profit = Revenue – COGS – Rent & Labour)
Correct: B. Revenue = ₹11.4 L. COGS = 40% × 11.4 = ₹4.56 L. Rent & Labour = ₹4.6 L. Operating Profit = 11.4 – 4.56 – 4.6 = ₹2.24 L/month. Compare: Express = 4.2 – (38% × 4.2) – 1.8 = 4.2 – 1.596 – 1.8 = ₹0.80 L. Lounge = 18.6 – (42% × 18.6) – 9.2 = 18.6 – 7.81 – 9.2 = ₹1.59 L. Drive-Thru has the highest per-outlet profitability.
Question 13 · Strategic Insight
UrbanSip's CEO wants to maximise return on invested capital (ROIC) in its next expansion of 30 new outlets. Based solely on the exhibit, which recommendation is best supported?
Correct: C. Drive-Thru wins on three key ROIC drivers: (1) highest YoY revenue growth at 31%, (2) highest operating profit per outlet at ₹2.24 L, and (3) moderate capex at ₹1.4 Cr vs ₹2.1 Cr for Lounge. Express has lower capex but far lower per-outlet profit (₹0.80 L). A strong answer would also note that Lounge's lower growth and higher capex make it less attractive for expansion, though it should be maintained for brand premium.
Question 14 · Customer Data
Which customer segment in the Lounge format generates the highest revenue per month per customer visiting?
Correct: C. Monthly revenue per customer = avg. visits/month × spend/visit. Remote workers: 12.1 × ₹620 = ₹7,502. Young professionals: 6.2 × ₹480 = ₹2,976. Students: 8.4 × ₹310 = ₹2,604. Families: 2.1 × ₹740 = ₹1,554. Remote workers win decisively despite being only 22% of the segment mix — a key insight for loyalty programme design.
Question 15 · Multi-select · Customer Insight
UrbanSip is designing a premium loyalty programme for Lounge customers. Based on the exhibit, select ALL customer segments that should be prioritised (multiple answers may be correct).
Select all that apply
Correct: A, B, C. Remote workers (C) are highest-value: ₹7,502/month, NPS +71. Young professionals (A) have strong frequency and high NPS. Students (B) visit 8.4×/month — the highest frequency — and can be upsold. Families (D) have the highest per-visit spend but visit only 2.1× per month, making them low-value loyalty targets (₹1,554/month). A loyalty programme should focus on high-frequency or high-value segments.
Question 16 · Exhibit Analysis
Express outlets serve 52% of total customers but generate a disproportionately lower share of revenue. The single most important structural reason for this, based on the exhibit, is:
Correct: B. Volume × Ticket Size = Revenue per outlet metric. Express: 78 customers/day × ₹180 = ₹14,040/day ≈ ₹4.2 L/month ✓. Lounge: 148 × ₹420 = ₹62,160/day ≈ ₹18.6 L ✓. The ticket size gap (₹180 vs ₹420) is the primary structural driver of the revenue disparity. More customers don't translate to more revenue when ticket size is less than half.
Question 17 · Exhibit Analysis
A new competitor is aggressively targeting Lounge-style cafés in metro cities. Which data point from the exhibit represents UrbanSip's STRONGEST competitive moat in the Lounge format?
Correct: B. A moat is a sustainable competitive advantage. High visit frequency (12.1×/month) combined with high NPS (+71) among remote workers indicates deeply entrenched behaviour — these customers have integrated UrbanSip into their daily work routine. This habitual usage is extremely hard for a new entrant to disrupt. Revenue share (A) and satisfaction (C) are outcomes, not moats. D is a flawed interpretation — low growth in Lounge reflects saturation of a mature format, which a competitor could target precisely.
Question 18 · Multi-select · Strategic Risks
Based on the exhibit, select ALL statements that represent genuine risks for UrbanSip going forward.
Select all that apply
Correct: A, B, D. A: Revenue concentration in 22% of outlets is a real risk. B: Express has low per-outlet profit with high outlet count — operational burden with thin returns. Question 19 · 50-Word Recommendation
Context: A profitable mid-sized Indian edtech company (₹400 Cr ARR, 18% EBITDA margin) is considering acquiring a loss-making but rapidly growing live-tutoring startup (₹80 Cr ARR, –25% EBITDA, 85% YoY growth) to enter the K–12 premium segment.

Task: In exactly 50 words, write your recommendation to the CEO on whether to proceed with the acquisition and the single most critical condition that must be met.
0 / 50 words
Evaluators look for: A clear stance (proceed / don't proceed / conditional), one specific and measurable condition (not vague), logical structure within 50 words. Top responses acknowledge the growth-profitability tension explicitly. Sample direction: "Conditionally recommend proceeding — the startup's 85% growth offers a credible K–12 entry that would take years to build organically. Critical condition: the path to EBITDA breakeven within 24 months must be clearly mapped; acquiring without a defined integration and monetisation plan risks capital erosion."
Question 20 · Prioritisation
A new CEO of a struggling airline has 6 months and limited capital. She faces four pressing issues simultaneously:

P1: Three high-traffic routes are operating at a loss due to aggressive competitor pricing
P2: Customer NPS has fallen to –12 due to delays and baggage handling failures
P3: Fuel hedging contracts expire in 8 weeks, exposing the airline to commodity risk
P4: A potential codeshare partner has signalled interest but needs a response within 30 days

Rank these priorities P1–P4 for the CEO's first 6 weeks, and justify your ranking in 3–4 lines.
0 words
Model prioritisation — P3 → P4 → P2 → P1: P3 is the most urgent non-negotiable: fuel cost exposure in 8 weeks is existential for a capital-constrained airline. P4 has a hard 30-day deadline and a codeshare can provide cash flow and network access. P2 (NPS) drives long-term loyalty and should be addressed in parallel with quick wins (baggage). P1 (loss-making routes) requires data before action — suspend, reprice, or exit decisions need analysis, not speed.

Question 21 · Guesstimate (Video Response)
Estimate the total annual revenue of the organised coffee café market in India in FY2025.

Your response will be evaluated as a video submission. Structure your thinking as follows:

① Define the market — what counts as "organised café"? ② Estimate the addressable population and filtering logic ③ Build up to a per-outlet or per-customer annual revenue figure ④ State your answer with a range and a sanity check

Evaluation rubric

Structure (30%): MECE segmentation, clear logical flow
Assumptions (30%): Reasonable, stated explicitly, defensible
Arithmetic (20%): Correct computation; errors flagged if caught
Sanity check (20%): Cross-validated against known benchmarks

Indicative answer range: ₹8,000 – ₹12,000 Cr. Approach: India's urban population ≈ 500 M. Café-going demographic (15–45 in Tier 1/2 cities) ≈ 120 M. Organised café penetration ≈ 15% visit at least monthly → 18 M regular customers. Avg. spend ₹300/visit × 2 visits/month × 12 = ₹7,200/year. Revenue = 18 M × ₹7,200 = ₹12,960 Cr. Sanity check: ~5,000–7,000 organised outlets (CCD, Starbucks, Blue Tokai, Barista, regional chains) × ₹15–20 L/month = ₹9,000–16,800 Cr/year. Reasonable range: ₹9,000–12,000 Cr.
